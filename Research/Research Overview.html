Part 1

Revised Research Methodology

Research Aim

This study develops a Burn Equity Index (BEI) that measures structural inequities in timely burn-care access. The BEI is designed as an access and capacity model, not a patient-outcome model and not a generic social vulnerability score. Its purpose is to evaluate where people face structural disadvantages in reaching specialized burn care under the constraints of available hospital infrastructure, travel burden, pediatric capability, and burn-bed capacity.

Conceptual Framing

The revised methodology is grounded in the idea that burn-care inequity is primarily a systems-access problem. Because the NIRD dataset is a hospital-level dataset focused on burn-center designation, geography, and bed resources, the index should reflect specialized supply, transfer-aware access, pediatric access, and structural capacity. Demand-side or exposure variables are treated separately as optional overlays rather than core components, since local burn-incidence estimates are not reliably available at fine geographic scales from public data alone.

Geographic Unit of Analysis

The recommended analytic unit is the census tract. This provides the best balance between spatial detail and feasibility because public datasets such as ACS, RUCA, and SVI are available at tract level, while census blocks are too fine and county-level analysis is too coarse to capture sub-county disparities. County, state, and regional results can then be produced by aggregating tract-level BEI scores.

Data Sources

The model uses challenge-provided NIRD + public/free augmentation layers for geographic augmentation, routing, and contextual analysis:

  • NIRD — burn-center status, trauma capability, pediatric services, and burn-bed counts
  • American Community Survey (ACS) — tract-level total population and child population
  • TIGER/Line shapefiles — census tract boundaries and centroids
  • Census Geocoder — assigning hospital locations to census geography
  • USDA RUCA codes — tract-level rural/urban classification
  • CDC Social Vulnerability Index (SVI) — optional contextual overlay
  • OpenStreetMap road network + OSRM — road-network travel times
  • FAA airport / heliport data (Airport Data & Information Portal) — optional air-access sensitivity scenarios

The public/free augmentation layers are verifiable, while NIRD should be described as challenge-provided unless an independent public bulk release source is later confirmed.

BEI Structure

The BEI is computed as a weighted composite of four structural dimensions:

\[ \mathrm{BEI}_i \;=\; 100\left(\,0.25\,S_i \;+\; 0.30\,T_i \;+\; 0.20\,P_i \;+\; 0.25\,C_i\,\right) \]

where \(S_i\) = specialized supply scarcity, \(T_i\) = timely access burden, \(P_i\) = pediatric access gap, \(C_i\) = structural capacity gaphigher BEI indicates worse structural inequity in timely burn-care access.

Component Definitions

1. Specialized Supply Scarcity \(S_i\)

Measures how limited access is to specialized burn-care facilities relative to the local population. Facilities are weighted by capability, with the greatest weight given to ABA-verified burn centers, followed by state-designated centers, burn-capable non-verified hospitals, and trauma-only stabilization sites. Accessibility is computed using a travel-time decay function.

2. Timely Access Burden \(T_i\)

Captures the travel burden to definitive burn care, incorporating both direct access to the nearest burn center and transfer-aware access, where a patient may first go to a local stabilization hospital before transfer. This reflects the reality of regionalized burn systems, especially in rural areas.

3. Pediatric Access Gap \(P_i\)

Modeled separately because pediatric burn access is a specific challenge requirement and a distinct systems issue. Uses child population denominators and pediatric-capable facility indicators from NIRD.

4. Structural Capacity Gap \(C_i\)

Measures the adequacy of structural burn-bed capacity reachable from each tract using NIRD-reported BURN_BEDS. Explicitly avoids interpreting this as real-time open-bed availability. An optional conservative utilization factor may be applied in sensitivity analysis.

Accessibility Modelling Approach

The methodology uses an E2SFCA-style accessibility framework combined with a scenario-based transport model, accounting for distance decay, resource competition, multi-facility access, facility capability differences, referral and transfer structure, and ground-only vs. multimodal assumptions.

For any origin tract \(i\) and facility \(j\), let \(m \in \{\text{road},\,\text{air}\}\) denote transport mode. Define a mode-specific travel time \(t_{ij}^{(m)}\) where:

  • \(t_{ij}^{(\text{road})}\) = road-network time from OSRM / OpenStreetMap
  • \(t_{ij}^{(\text{air})}\) = total air-access time, not merely in-air flight time

The air pathway is decomposed as a multi-stage pipeline:

\[ t_{ij}^{(\text{air})} \;=\; t_i^{\text{dispatch}} \;+\; t_i^{\text{ground-to-launch}} \;+\; t_{ij}^{\text{flight}} \;+\; t_j^{\text{landing-to-facility}} \;+\; t_{ij}^{\text{handoff}} \]

Because nationwide real-time air-ambulance dispatch availability is not publicly available, the air term is used as a scenario-based sensitivity analysis, not a claim about guaranteed helicopter access.

To avoid overstating air access, define a feasibility rule for each scenario:

\[ \tilde{t}_{ij}^{(m)} \;=\; \begin{cases} t_{ij}^{(m)}, & \text{if mode }m\text{ is allowed under the scenario} \\[4pt] +\infty, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

Direct access to definitive burn care:

\[ T_i^{\text{dir}} \;=\; \min_{d\,\in\,\mathcal{D}} \!\left[\, \min_{m\,\in\,\{\text{road},\text{air}\}} \tilde{t}_{id}^{(m)} \,\right] \]

Transfer-aware access (patient goes first to stabilization hospital \(s\), then to burn center \(d\)):

\[ T_i^{\text{trans}} \;=\; \min_{\substack{s\,\in\,\mathcal{S} \\ d\,\in\,\mathcal{D}}} \!\left[\, \min_{m_1\,\in\,\{\text{road},\text{air}\}} \tilde{t}_{is}^{(m_1)} \;+\; \tau_s \;+\; \min_{m_2\,\in\,\{\text{road},\text{air}\}} \tilde{t}_{sd}^{(m_2)} \,\right] \]

Overall system access time:

\[ T_i^{\text{sys}} \;=\; \min\!\left(T_i^{\text{dir}},\; T_i^{\text{trans}}\right) \]

Transport Scenarios and Public-Data Feasibility

The recommended reporting structure publishes at least two scenarios:

  1. Ground-only baseline — OpenStreetMap + OSRM road-network routing
  2. Conditional ground-plus-air sensitivity — public FAA airport/heliport infrastructure data plus analyst-defined dispatch and handoff assumptions

A practical implementation:

  • Use OpenStreetMap / Geofabrik extracts and OSRM for all surface legs
  • Use FAA airport / heliport records for candidate launch and landing infrastructure
  • Estimate flight segment from coordinates using a transparent assumed cruise-speed parameter
  • Keep dispatch and handoff times as explicit scenario parameters

Real-time rotor-wing availability, weather restrictions, and operational dispatch rules are not directly observed in the public stack.

Treatment of Rurality and Social Context

RUCA codes stratify rural vs. urban access burdens. SVI and social context variables are not part of the core BEI — they serve as a secondary lens for interpretation or hotspot prioritization.

Need and Exposure Overlay

The methodology does not include burn exposure or local injury burden as a core BEI pillar, since public datasets do not reliably support small-area burn-incidence estimation. Need is presented as a separate overlay using total population, child population, and optionally a validated injury proxy.

Aggregation and Reporting

BEI is computed at the tract level and reported at multiple levels:

  • Tract-level maps for hotspots and fine-grained disparities
  • County-level summaries using population-weighted averages
  • State / regional summaries for policy comparison

Challenge-specific outputs reported directly: burn centers per capita, rural vs. urban travel burden, pediatric access relative to child population, structural burn-bed capacity in high- vs. low-need areas.

Methodological Contribution

The novelty lies in reframing burn inequity as a structural access and capacity problem. Key contributions:

  • Tract-level structural equity index
  • Transfer-aware access pathways
  • Explicit separation of pediatric access
  • Distinction between structural capacity and real-time availability
  • Preservation of regionalized, tiered care logic

Final Methodological Position

The revised BEI methodology is a tract-based, literature-informed structural access model using challenge-provided NIRD + public/free augmentation layers (ACS, TIGER/Line, Census Geocoder, RUCA, SVI, OpenStreetMap/OSRM, optional FAA infrastructure). It quantifies inequity in access to specialized burn care through four core dimensions: supply, timely access, pediatric access, and structural capacity.

Part 2

BEI — Implementation-Ready Specification

A detailed, implementation-ready BEI specification consistent with the revised framing: structural access and capacity at census-tract level.

1) Core BEI Definition

Let \(i\) index a census tract.

\[ \boxed{ \mathrm{BEI}_i \;=\; 100\Bigl(0.25\,S_i \;+\; 0.30\,T_i \;+\; 0.20\,P_i \;+\; 0.25\,C_i\Bigr) } \]

with:

  • \(S_i\) = specialized supply scarcity
  • \(T_i\) = timely access burden
  • \(P_i\) = pediatric access gap
  • \(C_i\) = structural capacity gap

Each component is scaled to \([0,1]\): \(0\) = best structural access, \(1\) = worst. Higher BEI = worse structural inequity in timely burn-care access.

2) Geography, Sets, and Notation

Index sets

  • \(i \in \mathcal{I}\) — census tracts
  • \(j \in \mathcal{J}\) — hospitals / facilities from NIRD
  • \(d \in \mathcal{D} \subseteq \mathcal{J}\) — definitive burn centers
  • \(s \in \mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{J}\) — stabilization-capable hospitals

Population terms

  • \(\mathrm{Pop}_i\) — total tract population
  • \(\mathrm{ChildPop}_i\) — tract child population

Transport sets and scenarios

  • \(m \in \mathcal{M} = \{\text{road},\,\text{air}\}\) — transport mode
  • \(\omega \in \Omega\) — transport scenario (e.g. ground-only or conditional ground-plus-air)

Travel times

  • \(t_{ij}^{(\text{road})}\) — road-network travel time, tract \(i\) to facility \(j\)
  • \(t_{sd}^{(\text{road})}\) — road-network travel time, stabilization hospital \(s\) to burn center \(d\)
  • \(t_{ij}^{(\text{air})}\) — total air-access time, tract \(i\) to facility \(j\)
  • \(t_{sd}^{(\text{air})}\) — total air-access time, stabilization hospital \(s\) to burn center \(d\)
  • \(\tilde{t}_{ij}^{(m,\omega)}\) — scenario-specific feasible travel time for leg \((i,j)\) under mode \(m\)
  • \(\tilde{t}_{sd}^{(m,\omega)}\) — scenario-specific feasible travel time for leg \((s,d)\) under mode \(m\)

Feasible mode-specific time — for any origin-destination pair \((a,b)\):

\[ \tilde{t}_{ab}^{(m,\omega)} \;=\; \begin{cases} t_{ab}^{(m)}, & \text{if mode }m\text{ is allowed under scenario }\omega \\[4pt] +\infty, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

Air-access decomposition

Air access is treated as a multi-stage pathway, not just straight-line flight time:

\[ t_{ab}^{(\text{air})} \;=\; t_a^{\text{dispatch}} \;+\; t_a^{\text{ground-to-launch}} \;+\; t_{ab}^{\text{flight}} \;+\; t_b^{\text{landing-to-facility}} \;+\; t_{ab}^{\text{handoff}} \]

Road legs are computed from OpenStreetMap + OSRM; candidate airport/heliport infrastructure sourced from public FAA records.

Transfer penalty

\(\tau_s\) is a structural routing penalty — not patient-level waiting time — representing the extra burden of requiring interfacility transfer. For a first implementation set \(\tau_s = \tau\) for all \(s\), where \(\tau\) is a constant (e.g. 30, 45, or 60 minutes), then test sensitivity:

\[ \tau_s \;=\; \tau \quad \forall\, s \in \mathcal{S} \]

3) Distance-Decay Function

A step-decay function is recommended for transparency and ease of explanation:

\[ g(t) \;=\; \begin{cases} 1.00, & t \;\leq\; 30 \\[3pt] 0.60, & 30 \;\lt\; t \;\leq\; 60 \\[3pt] 0.30, & 60 \;\lt\; t \;\leq\; 90 \\[3pt] 0, & t \;\gt\; 90 \end{cases} \]

This gives a primary 60-minute interpretation while still distinguishing 30-minute and 90-minute sensitivity bands.

4) Robust Normalization

For any raw metric \(X_i\), define robust min-max normalization using national tract percentiles:

\[ \mathrm{Norm}(X_i) \;=\; \min\!\left\{ 1,\;\; \max\!\left[ 0,\;\; \frac{X_i - Q_5(X)}{Q_{95}(X) - Q_5(X)} \right] \right\} \]

where \(Q_5(X)\) and \(Q_{95}(X)\) are the 5th and 95th percentiles of the raw tract distribution. This prevents extreme outliers from dominating the score.

For metrics where higher raw values are better, convert to a gap:

\[ \mathrm{Gap}(X_i) \;=\; 1 - \mathrm{Norm}(X_i) \]

5) Component Formulas

A. Specialized Supply Scarcity \(S_i\)

Measures how scarce specialized burn-care supply is around tract \(i\) using a weighted E2SFCA accessibility score.

A1. Facility capability weight

\[ q_j^{(S)} \;=\; \begin{cases} 1.00, & \text{ABA verified burn center} \\[3pt] 0.85, & \text{state-designated burn center} \\[3pt] 0.50, & \text{burn-capable non-verified center} \\[3pt] 0.20, & \text{trauma-only stabilization site} \\[3pt] 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

A2. Facility-side provider-to-population ratio

\[ R_j^{(S)} \;=\; \frac{q_j^{(S)}}{\displaystyle\sum_{k\,\in\,\mathcal{I}} \mathrm{Pop}_k\; g(t_{kj})} \]

A3. Tract-side specialized supply accessibility

\[ A_i^{(S)} \;=\; \sum_{j\,\in\,\mathcal{J}} R_j^{(S)}\; g(t_{ij}) \]

A4. Convert to scarcity score

\[ \boxed{S_i \;=\; 1 - \mathrm{Norm}\!\left(A_i^{(S)}\right)} \]

Large \(A_i^{(S)}\) = strong nearby specialty supply; large \(S_i\) = scarce specialty supply.

B. Timely Access Burden \(T_i\)

Measures the burden of reaching definitive burn care under a regionalized, tiered system. Use scenario-based multimodal routing and publish at least a ground-only baseline and a conditional ground-plus-air sensitivity scenario.

B1. Direct travel time to definitive burn care

\[ T^{\text{dir}}_{i,\omega} \;=\; \min_{d\,\in\,\mathcal{D}} \!\left[\; \min_{m\,\in\,\mathcal{M}} \tilde{t}_{id}^{(m,\omega)} \;\right] \]

B2. Travel time to nearest stabilization-capable hospital

\[ T^{\text{stab}}_{i,\omega} \;=\; \min_{s\,\in\,\mathcal{S}} \!\left[\; \min_{m\,\in\,\mathcal{M}} \tilde{t}_{is}^{(m,\omega)} \;\right] \]

B3. Transfer-aware definitive care path

\[ T^{\text{trans}}_{i,\omega} \;=\; \min_{\substack{s\,\in\,\mathcal{S} \\ d\,\in\,\mathcal{D}}} \!\left[\; \min_{m_1\,\in\,\mathcal{M}} \tilde{t}_{is}^{(m_1,\omega)} \;+\; \tau_s \;+\; \min_{m_2\,\in\,\mathcal{M}} \tilde{t}_{sd}^{(m_2,\omega)} \;\right] \]

The first leg and second leg may use different feasible modes under the same scenario.

B4. System travel time

\[ T^{\text{sys}}_{i,\omega} \;=\; \min\!\left(T^{\text{dir}}_{i,\omega},\;\; T^{\text{trans}}_{i,\omega}\right) \]

The system can function either via direct definitive access or via stabilization followed by transfer.

B5. Tier penalty

Penalizes places that lack prompt stabilization even if a transfer pathway technically exists:

\[ \Delta_{i,\omega} \;=\; \max\!\left(0,\;\; T^{\text{stab}}_{i,\omega} - 30\right) \]

No penalty if stabilization is within 30 minutes; increasing penalty beyond that.

B6. Timely access burden score

\[ \boxed{ T_{i,\omega} \;=\; 0.75\;\mathrm{Norm}\!\left(T^{\text{sys}}_{i,\omega}\right) \;+\; 0.25\;\mathrm{Norm}\!\left(\Delta_{i,\omega}\right) } \]

First term = overall system burden to definitive care; second term = penalty for weak tiered infrastructure.

B7. Public-data implementation for road + air

  • Road legs: OpenStreetMap road data + OSRM route/table services
  • Air infrastructure: public FAA airport / heliport records
  • Flight segment: coordinate-based distance ÷ documented scenario cruise speed
  • Dispatch / handoff: explicit analyst-chosen scenario constants
  • Feasibility rule: air only allowed when origin and destination are linked to plausible launch / landing infrastructure

C. Pediatric Access Gap \(P_i\)

C1. Pediatric capability weight

\[ q_j^{(P)} \;=\; \begin{cases} 1.00, & \text{pediatric burn capable and ABA verified} \\[3pt] 0.85, & \text{pediatric burn capable and state-designated} \\[3pt] 0.60, & \text{pediatric trauma L1/L2 plus burn-capable} \\[3pt] 0.25, & \text{pediatric stabilization only} \\[3pt] 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

Mapped from NIRD fields: BURN_PEDS, PEDS_TRAUMA_L1, PEDS_TRAUMA_L2, ABA_VERIFIED, BC_STATE_DESIGNATED.

C2. Facility-side pediatric provider-to-child-population ratio

\[ R_j^{(P)} \;=\; \frac{q_j^{(P)}}{\displaystyle\sum_{k\,\in\,\mathcal{I}} \mathrm{ChildPop}_k\; g(t_{kj})} \]

C3. Tract-side pediatric accessibility

\[ A_i^{(P)} \;=\; \sum_{j\,\in\,\mathcal{J}} R_j^{(P)}\; g(t_{ij}) \]

C4. Convert to pediatric access gap

\[ \boxed{P_i \;=\; 1 - \mathrm{Norm}\!\left(A_i^{(P)}\right)} \]

High \(P_i\) = worse pediatric burn access relative to child population.

D. Structural Capacity Gap \(C_i\)

D1. Effective structural bed count

\[ \mathrm{EffBeds}_j \;=\; \mathrm{BURN\_BEDS}_j \cdot u_j \]

Baseline structural scenario: \(u_j = 1\). Conservative sensitivity: \(u_j = u\) with \(u \in [0.6,\,0.9]\). This does not claim real-time occupancy truth.

D2. Facility-side bed-to-population ratio

\[ R_j^{(C)} \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{EffBeds}_j\; q_j^{(S)}}{\displaystyle\sum_{k\,\in\,\mathcal{I}} \mathrm{Pop}_k\; g(t_{kj})} \]

D3. Tract-side structural bed accessibility

\[ A_i^{(C)} \;=\; \sum_{j\,\in\,\mathcal{J}} R_j^{(C)}\; g(t_{ij}) \]

D4. Convert to capacity gap

\[ \boxed{C_i \;=\; 1 - \mathrm{Norm}\!\left(A_i^{(C)}\right)} \]

High \(C_i\) = poor structural access to burn-bed capacity.

6) Final BEI — Full Expanded Form

\[ \boxed{ \mathrm{BEI}_i \;=\; 100\,\Bigl[ \;0.25\Bigl(1-\mathrm{Norm}(A_i^{(S)})\Bigr) \;+\; 0.30\Bigl(0.75\;\mathrm{Norm}(T_{i,\omega}^{\text{sys}})+0.25\;\mathrm{Norm}(\Delta_{i,\omega})\Bigr) \;+\; 0.20\Bigl(1-\mathrm{Norm}(A_i^{(P)})\Bigr) \;+\; 0.25\Bigl(1-\mathrm{Norm}(A_i^{(C)})\Bigr) \;\Bigr] } \]

where the sub-expressions are:

\[ A_i^{(S)} \;=\; \sum_{j\,\in\,\mathcal{J}} \left(\frac{q_j^{(S)}}{\displaystyle\sum_{k\,\in\,\mathcal{I}}\mathrm{Pop}_k\,g(t_{kj})}\right) g(t_{ij}) \]
\[ T_{i,\omega}^{\text{sys}} \;=\; \min\!\left( \min_{d\,\in\,\mathcal{D}}\;\min_{m\,\in\,\mathcal{M}}\,\tilde{t}_{id}^{(m,\omega)},\;\; \min_{\substack{s\,\in\,\mathcal{S}\\d\,\in\,\mathcal{D}}} \!\left( \min_{m_1\,\in\,\mathcal{M}} \tilde{t}_{is}^{(m_1,\omega)} +\tau_s+ \min_{m_2\,\in\,\mathcal{M}} \tilde{t}_{sd}^{(m_2,\omega)} \right) \right) \]
\[ \Delta_{i,\omega} \;=\; \max\!\left(0,\; T_{i,\omega}^{\text{stab}}-30\right) \qquad\text{with}\qquad T_{i,\omega}^{\text{stab}} \;=\; \min_{s\,\in\,\mathcal{S}}\;\min_{m\,\in\,\mathcal{M}}\,\tilde{t}_{is}^{(m,\omega)} \]
\[ A_i^{(P)} \;=\; \sum_{j\,\in\,\mathcal{J}} \left(\frac{q_j^{(P)}}{\displaystyle\sum_{k\,\in\,\mathcal{I}}\mathrm{ChildPop}_k\,g(t_{kj})}\right) g(t_{ij}) \]
\[ A_i^{(C)} \;=\; \sum_{j\,\in\,\mathcal{J}} \left(\frac{\mathrm{EffBeds}_j\;q_j^{(S)}}{\displaystyle\sum_{k\,\in\,\mathcal{I}}\mathrm{Pop}_k\,g(t_{kj})}\right) g(t_{ij}) \]

7) Companion Outputs to Publish beside BEI

Report these separately so judges can see concrete challenge outputs alongside the composite score.

\[ \mathrm{NearestBurnTime}_{i,\omega} \;=\; \min_{d\,\in\,\mathcal{D}}\;\min_{m\,\in\,\mathcal{M}}\,\tilde{t}_{id}^{(m,\omega)} \]
\[ \mathrm{BedsPer100k}_i \;=\; 100{,}000\cdot \frac{\displaystyle\sum_j \mathrm{EffBeds}_j\;g(t_{ij})}{\mathrm{Pop}_i} \]
\[ \mathrm{PedsAccess}_i \;=\; A_i^{(P)} \]
\[ \mathrm{CentersPer100k}_i \;=\; 100{,}000\cdot \frac{\displaystyle\sum_j q_j^{(S)}\,g(t_{ij})}{\mathrm{Pop}_i} \]

For county \(m\), use population-weighted rollups:

\[ \mathrm{BEI}_m \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i\,\in\,m}\mathrm{Pop}_i\;\mathrm{BEI}_i} {\displaystyle\sum_{i\,\in\,m}\mathrm{Pop}_i} \]

8) Need Overlay — Kept Separate from BEI

Because BEI is a structural access and capacity measure, demand uncertainty is not folded into the core score. Define a separate priority overlay if needed:

\[ \mathrm{NeedOverlay}_i \;=\; \alpha\;\mathrm{Norm}(\mathrm{Pop}_i) \;+\; (1-\alpha)\;\mathrm{Norm}(\mathrm{ChildPop}_i) \]

Then a planning-layer priority score:

\[ \mathrm{Priority}_i \;=\; \mathrm{BEI}_i \;\times\; \bigl(1 + \lambda\;\mathrm{NeedOverlay}_i\bigr) \]

Keep this outside the core BEI.

9) Recommended Default Parameter Choices

  • Catchment bands: 30 / 60 / 90 minutes
  • Primary policy threshold: 60 minutes
  • Stabilization threshold in \(\Delta_{i,\omega}\): 30 minutes
  • Transfer penalty \(\tau\): 45 minutes (baseline)
  • Baseline scenario: ground-only
  • Multimodal sensitivity scenario: conditional ground-plus-air
  • Air dispatch and handoff terms: explicit scenario constants
  • Air flight segment: coordinate-based distance ÷ documented scenario cruise speed
  • Capacity utilization factor \(u\): 1.0 (baseline), 0.75 (sensitivity)
  • Normalization: 5th–95th percentile winsorized min-max

10) Public/Free Data Scope for the Transport Model

The road portion is feasible with public/free sources:

  • OpenStreetMap road network data
  • OSRM route/table services

The air sensitivity scenario is feasible with public/free infrastructure data:

  • Public FAA airport / heliport records for launch and landing infrastructure
  • Tract and hospital coordinates from Census geographies / geocoding
  • Analyst-defined dispatch, handoff, and cruise-speed parameters

What is not directly observed: real-time rotor-wing availability, weather constraints, live dispatch logic. The air component must therefore be reported as a scenario-based sensitivity analysis.

11) Plain-English Interpretation

A tract gets a high BEI if it has:

  • Weak nearby specialized burn supply
  • Long direct or transfer-aware travel burden
  • Poor pediatric-specific access
  • Weak structural access to burn-bed capacity

A tract gets a low BEI if it has:

  • Strong nearby specialty supply
  • Timely direct or regionalized transfer access
  • Good pediatric access
  • Strong structural bed access
