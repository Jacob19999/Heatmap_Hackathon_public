Methodology

How the Burn Equity Index is calculated and what it measures

What is the Burn Equity Index?

The Burn Equity Index (BEI) is a composite score that measures structural inequity in access to burn care for every census tract in the United States. It does not predict who will get burned or how hospitals perform day-to-day. Instead, it asks: given where people live and where burn-care resources are located, how equitably is the system structured?

The score ranges from 0 to 100. A higher score means worse structural access — more barriers stand between residents of that area and timely, specialized burn treatment.

0 — Best Access 50 100 — Worst Access

The Core Formula

The BEI is a weighted average of four pillars, each measuring a different dimension of burn-care access. Each pillar is scored from 0 (best) to 1 (worst), and the composite is scaled to 0–100:

$$ \mathrm{BEI}_i = 100 \Big( 0.25\,S_i + 0.30\,T_i + 0.20\,P_i + 0.25\,C_i \Big) $$

In plain terms: for each census tract, we combine four gap scores — supply scarcity, travel burden, pediatric access, and bed capacity — using fixed weights that reflect their relative importance.

S
Specialized Supply ScarcityWeight: 25%

How scarce is specialized burn-care supply near this area? Areas far from verified burn centers with few nearby resources score higher (worse).

T
Timely Access BurdenWeight: 30%

How long does it take to reach definitive burn care — either directly or through stabilization and transfer? This is the most heavily weighted pillar.

P
Pediatric Access GapWeight: 20%

How well can children in this area access burn care designed for pediatric patients? Areas without nearby pediatric-capable facilities score higher.

C
Structural Capacity GapWeight: 25%

Are there enough burn beds to serve the surrounding population? This measures bed-to-population ratios weighted by distance, not real-time occupancy.

How Each Pillar is Calculated

Three of the four pillars (Supply, Pediatric, Capacity) use the same approach — a two-step floating catchment area method. This is a standard technique in health-access research. Here is how it works in plain language:

1
Measure facility burden. For each hospital, calculate how much demand it faces by summing up the populations of all nearby tracts (weighted by how close they are). Divide the hospital's capability score by this demand. Hospitals that serve many people spread their capacity thin.
2
Sum accessibility for each tract. For each census tract, add up the burden-adjusted scores of all hospitals it can reach (again weighted by distance). The result is that tract's raw accessibility score.
3
Normalize and flip. Normalize the raw score across all tracts, then subtract from 1 so that higher values mean worse access (a "gap").

S Specialized Supply Scarcity

This pillar asks: how much specialized burn-care capability exists near each tract, relative to the population competing for it?

Each facility receives a capability weight based on its designation:

Facility TypeWeight
ABA Verified Burn Center1.00
State-Designated Burn Center0.85
Burn-Capable Non-Verified Center0.50
Trauma-Only Stabilization Site0.20

The two-step floating catchment method produces a raw accessibility score. Higher raw scores mean more nearby supply. We normalize and invert it:

$$ S_i = 1 - \mathrm{Norm}\!\left( A_i^{(S)} \right) $$
$$ A_i^{(S)} = \sum_{j} \frac{q_j^{(S)}}{\sum_{k}\, \mathrm{Pop}_k \; g(t_{kj})} \;\cdot\; g(t_{ij}) $$

A tract with high $S_i$ has little specialized burn supply nearby relative to its surrounding population.

T Timely Access Burden

This pillar models how the real burn-care system works: a patient may go directly to a verified burn center, or they may first go to a closer stabilization hospital and then be transferred. The index picks whichever pathway is faster.

A
Direct path: drive time from the tract to the nearest definitive burn center.
B
Transfer path: drive to the nearest stabilization hospital + a fixed transfer penalty (45 min) + drive/air from that hospital to a definitive center.
C
System time: take the minimum of the direct and transfer paths — the fastest realistic option.
$$ T_{i}^{\text{sys}} = \min\!\Big( T_i^{\text{dir}},\;\; T_i^{\text{trans}} \Big) $$

A second term penalizes tracts where even first-line stabilization is far away (more than 30 minutes):

$$ \Delta_i = \max\!\big(0,\; T_i^{\text{stab}} - 30\big) $$

The final travel burden score blends both:

$$ T_i = 0.75 \;\mathrm{Norm}\!\left(T_i^{\text{sys}}\right) + 0.25 \;\mathrm{Norm}\!\left(\Delta_i\right) $$

In plain terms: 75% of the travel score comes from overall system travel time, and 25% from whether there is a stabilization hospital within 30 minutes.

P Pediatric Access Gap

Children require specialized burn care. This pillar uses the same two-step method as Supply Scarcity, but focuses on pediatric-capable facilities and weights them against the child population (under 18) instead of total population.

Pediatric Facility TypeWeight
Pediatric Burn Capable + ABA Verified1.00
Pediatric Burn Capable + State-Designated0.85
Pediatric Trauma L1/L2 + Burn-Capable0.60
Pediatric Stabilization Only0.25
$$ P_i = 1 - \mathrm{Norm}\!\left( A_i^{(P)} \right) $$

A tract with high $P_i$ has poor access to pediatric burn care relative to the number of children living nearby.

C Structural Capacity Gap

This pillar measures whether there are enough burn beds to serve the surrounding population, weighted by distance. It uses each facility's reported burn-bed count, adjusted by its capability weight.

$$ C_i = 1 - \mathrm{Norm}\!\left( A_i^{(C)} \right) $$
$$ A_i^{(C)} = \sum_{j} \frac{\mathrm{EffBeds}_j \;\cdot\; q_j^{(S)}}{\sum_{k}\, \mathrm{Pop}_k \; g(t_{kj})} \;\cdot\; g(t_{ij}) $$

A tract with high $C_i$ has poor structural access to burn-bed capacity — few beds relative to the population that needs them.

Distance Decay: How "Nearby" is Defined

Not all facilities within reach matter equally. A hospital 10 minutes away contributes more to access than one 80 minutes away. We model this with a step-decay function based on drive time:

Drive TimeWeightMeaning
≤ 30 min1.00Full access — within ideal reach
31–60 min0.60Reduced access — reachable but strained
61–90 min0.30Marginal access — significant barrier
> 90 min0.00Out of effective reach

The 60-minute mark is the primary policy threshold, consistent with national burn-care guidelines recommending transfer within one hour.

Normalization: Making Scores Comparable

Raw values for each metric vary widely. To make them comparable, we use robust min-max normalization based on the 5th and 95th percentiles across all tracts nationally:

$$ \mathrm{Norm}(X_i) = \min\!\left(1,\; \max\!\left[0,\; \frac{X_i - Q_5}{Q_{95} - Q_5}\right]\right) $$

This clips extreme outliers so a few unusual tracts do not distort the entire scale. Values below the 5th percentile become 0; values above the 95th become 1.

For metrics where a higher raw value means better access, we flip the score: $\;\mathrm{Gap} = 1 - \mathrm{Norm}(X_i)$.

Full Expanded Formula

Substituting all component formulas into the core BEI equation:

$$ \mathrm{BEI}_i = 100 \bigg[ 0.25\Big(1 - \mathrm{Norm}(A_i^{(S)})\Big) + 0.30\Big(0.75\,\mathrm{Norm}(T_i^{\text{sys}}) + 0.25\,\mathrm{Norm}(\Delta_i)\Big) + 0.20\Big(1 - \mathrm{Norm}(A_i^{(P)})\Big) + 0.25\Big(1 - \mathrm{Norm}(A_i^{(C)})\Big) \bigg] $$

Interpreting the Score

High BEI (Worse Access)

  • Few specialized burn centers nearby
  • Long travel times — even with transfers
  • Poor pediatric burn-care access for children
  • Few burn beds relative to population

Low BEI (Better Access)

  • Strong nearby specialty supply
  • Timely direct or transfer-based access
  • Good pediatric burn-care availability
  • Adequate burn-bed capacity nearby

Data Sources

1
NIRD (National Inpatient Rehabilitation Database): Burn center locations, ABA verification status, bed counts, pediatric capability, and trauma level designations.
2
U.S. Census Bureau: Census tract boundaries, total and child population estimates, and geographic identifiers.
3
OpenStreetMap + OSRM: Road-network travel times between tract centroids and facility locations.
4
CDC/ATSDR SVI: Social Vulnerability Index for overlay analysis (kept separate from the core BEI score).

County-Level Rollup

The BEI is computed at the census-tract level. To produce county-level scores (used in the National map), we take a population-weighted average of all tracts within each county:

$$ \mathrm{BEI}_{\text{county}} = \frac{\sum_{i \in \text{county}} \mathrm{Pop}_i \times \mathrm{BEI}_i}{\sum_{i \in \text{county}} \mathrm{Pop}_i} $$

This ensures that the county score reflects the experience of the population, not just the average of land areas.

Limitations

Known constraints and caveats of the current Burn Equity Index implementation

Driving Distance Computation

The most significant limitation of this analysis is the driving distance and travel time calculation. All road-network routing was performed using Valhalla (an open-source routing engine built on OpenStreetMap data) running locally. This introduced two major constraints:

MN
Minnesota — Tract-Level RoutingHigher granularity

For Minnesota, driving distances were computed at the census-tract level — routing from every tract centroid to every relevant facility. This produces the most accurate travel time estimates and is the intended resolution for the BEI.

US
National — County-Level RoutingLower granularity

For the national analysis, driving distances were computed at the county level only (county centroid to facilities). Tract-level routing for the entire U.S. was infeasible due to RAM limitations on the local machine and the total query time required by Valhalla.

County-level routing means that all tracts within a county share the same driving distance estimate. In large or geographically diverse counties, this can mask significant within-county variation — for example, a tract at the far edge of a large rural county may be meaningfully farther from care than the county centroid suggests.

Hardware & Query Constraints

Valhalla's local tile server requires the full OSM road graph to be loaded into memory. For a nationwide origin–destination matrix at the tract level, this exceeds the available RAM on a consumer-grade workstation. Additionally, even at county resolution, the national query set (3,000+ counties × multiple facilities) required substantial processing time.

1
RAM bottleneck: Loading the full U.S. OSM graph into Valhalla required more memory than the local machine could provide for tract-level queries nationwide.
2
Query time: Even at county granularity, the full national routing matrix took significant wall-clock time to compute. Tract-level resolution (~73,000 origins) was not feasible within the hackathon timeframe.
3
Ideal scenario: With access to a high-memory server or a cloud-hosted routing API, tract-level travel times could be computed nationally, which would substantially improve the precision of the national BEI.

Missing Data: Florida

Florida counties are absent from the national map. During the Valhalla routing batch, queries for Florida origins returned errors and failed to produce valid travel-time results. Due to hackathon time constraints, there was insufficient time to diagnose and re-run the Florida routing queries.

FL
Florida — Missing from National BEIValhalla query error

Florida's 67 counties do not have BEI scores in the current dataset. This is a data-pipeline gap, not a methodological exclusion — the BEI formula applies identically to Florida. Given more time, re-running the Valhalla queries would fill this gap with no changes to the index methodology.
